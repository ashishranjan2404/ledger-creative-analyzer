# Your agent trace is not a log, and your log pipeline is going to lie to you about it

## Outline (pre-draft)

- Hook: the specific incident where an IR agent "resolved" a page at 2:47am and the post-mortem couldn't reconstruct why, because the trace got flattened into Loki as structured log lines.
- Claim (by end of §2): the ops industry is shipping agentic IR pipelines on top of an observability stack that was designed for request-response systems, and the impedance mismatch is the next class of silent failure. The fix is not "more logs." It is treating agent runs as first-class traces with branch and retry semantics, which your current tooling does not support.
- Evidence 1: OpenTelemetry GenAI semantic conventions (still incubating at time of writing) explicitly model `gen_ai.operation.name` and tool-call spans as nested trace spans, not log records. Adopting them after the fact is a migration, not a config flip.
- Evidence 2: the 2025 Honeycomb and Datadog write-ups on "LLM observability" both hit the same wall: flat span lists don't express branch/retry/backtrack, and their customers' first three post-mortems after deploying agents all blamed the wrong component.
- Counterargument: "I already log every tool call. Why is that not enough?" Steelman it, then answer: log records don't carry causal parents. You can't reconstruct a 14-step agent run from interleaved log lines across four services without doing it by hand, and nobody does it by hand at 3am.
- Takeaway: before you deploy another agent to prod, audit one thing — can you, from your existing tooling, answer "which tool call's output caused the agent to pick the wrong remediation" in under 2 minutes? If not, fix that before the next rollout, not after the first incident.

## The post

At 2:47am on a Tuesday my pager went off for a database failover that had already resolved itself. The resolution message said the on-call agent had restarted the stuck follower, confirmed replication lag was back under 200ms, and closed the page. I said thanks and went back to sleep.

In the morning I went to find out what had actually happened, because "restart the follower" is a remediation I do not want an agent running without me looking. I pulled the agent's trace out of our observability stack. What I got back was 380 structured log lines across four services, interleaved, with a `run_id` field but no causal parents, no branch markers, and no record of which of the eight tool calls the agent had *considered* before picking restart. The agent's reasoning was gone. The fact of its reasoning was not gone; every `kubectl describe` and every Prometheus query was there, timestamped to the millisecond. But the graph — the thing that would tell me "it looked at replica lag, then at disk I/O, then branched on the I/O reading, then decided" — was not recoverable, because the tool we used to ship it had flattened everything into a log stream and the stream does not carry parents.

The claim I'll make in the next sentence is the claim of the post: we are shipping agentic incident-response pipelines on top of observability infrastructure that was designed in 2016 for request-response services, and the impedance mismatch is going to be the next class of silent failure for SRE teams that take the leap. I don't think this is controversial if you have shipped an agent to prod, and I think it is underweighted in the discourse if you have not.

The vendors and the OSS working groups know this. The OpenTelemetry GenAI semantic conventions, incubating as of their 0.3 release with final status pending, explicitly model agent runs as *trace spans* with nested tool-call children, not as log records. `gen_ai.operation.name`, `gen_ai.tool.name`, `gen_ai.request.model`, and the retry/backtrack attributes are span attributes. [verify: the exact attribute name for backtrack across OTel 0.3 and 0.4] The semantics assume you carry a trace context through every LLM call, every tool invocation, and every retry, and that your backend can render the tree. Logs do not have a tree. You can fake one with correlation IDs, and you are going to find out the hard way that the fakery does not survive contact with a branched agent that retried twice, backtracked once, and parallelized three tool calls in the middle.

The second piece of evidence, if you haven't been following the vendor space: Honeycomb and Datadog both shipped LLM-observability products in 2025, and both have written publicly about the same wall they hit with design-partner customers. [verify: link to the Honeycomb post, I think it's their Nov 2025 "LLM tracing in practice" piece.] A flat list of spans does not express branch-and-retry. When a customer's first agent incident hits, the post-mortem wants to ask "which tool call's *output* caused the agent to pick the wrong plan?", and a flat span list cannot answer that question, because the causal link between output content and next-step selection is not in the span attributes. Datadog's public blog has a nearly identical observation. [verify] It is not a gap in one vendor's product. It is the shape of the problem.

The obvious counter is: "I already log every tool call, every model input, every model output. Isn't that enough? I can grep." The steelman is better than that. If you are running a well-instrumented service and you have the discipline to include `run_id` on every log line and you pipe it all to a single index, you can in principle reconstruct what happened. I have done it. It took me forty minutes for the 2:47am incident above, and at the end of forty minutes I still didn't have the causal graph. I had a hypothesis about the causal graph. The difference matters. A trace tree lets your eye follow "output of call 3 to input of decision 4" in about three seconds. A log stream with `run_id` requires you to do that join in your head, one hop at a time, while the next page is firing. You will not do this at 3am. Your junior on-call will not do this at 3am. The post-mortem will blame whichever subsystem is easiest to blame, and the real failure mode (the decision the agent made between step 7 and step 8) will live on unfixed until it happens again with a bigger blast radius.

There is a second counter worth addressing, which is that modern log backends (Loki, Elastic, Splunk) have been adding trace-like views for years, and those views are getting better. Fine. I'll grant that in 18 months the line between "log platform that supports traces" and "trace platform that supports logs" will be thinner than it is now. The argument isn't that logs are bad. The argument is that *your existing pipeline*, the one you already have instrumented, the one your team knows how to query, the one with 18 months of dashboards built on it, is shaped for request-response, and retrofitting it to ingest agent traces with branch semantics is not a weekend of YAML. It is a migration. You can do the migration. You should probably do the migration. You should not assume it is already done.

Here is the concrete thing you can do Monday morning. Pick one agent run from your staging or prod environment, any agent run, any tool, and sit with your on-call rotation and ask: from the tools we have right now, can the person who is on-call at 3am answer the question "which tool call's output caused the agent to take the next action it took" in under two minutes? Time it. If the answer is no, that is your observability debt, and it is sitting under every agent you ship. Fix it before you ship the next one. The fix is almost certainly a real OpenTelemetry GenAI trace emission path in the agent runtime, terminating at a backend that renders trees and not lists, with the trace context propagated through every tool boundary. It is not glamorous, it takes about three sprints depending on your stack, and it is the single highest-yield SRE investment you can make before the agentic-IR era happens to you whether you are ready or not.

I do not particularly want to run agents in my incident-response path. I have one now because the business case for it is good and the alternative, expecting five humans to hold a useful pager rotation for a product of our size, has worse economics. Given that the agent is going to be there, I would like to be able to debug it. My strong prior is that a lot of teams that are about to deploy agents to prod are going to discover, around week four, that they cannot. The question worth asking now, before you are the team in question, is whether your observability stack has trace semantics that survive branch and retry, or whether it has a very nice-looking list of log lines. Those are not the same artifact, and in 2026 the cost of mistaking the second for the first is going to show up in post-mortems.
