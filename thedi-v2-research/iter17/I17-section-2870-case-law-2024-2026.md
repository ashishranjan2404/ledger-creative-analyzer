## Executive Summary — The Short Version

- **The case-law landscape has NOT materially shifted since iter-2 D3 was drafted.** The dominant modern precedent remains *Whitewater West Industries, Ltd. v. Alleshouse*, 981 F.3d 1045 (Fed. Cir. 2020) — a 2020 decision that is still good law and is still the most-cited post-2020 case in law-firm client alerts on this topic. No published California appellate or Supreme Court decision in the 2024–2026 window has directly reinterpreted §2870's "relate to the employer's business / demonstrably anticipated R&D" test.
- **The single most useful 2024-2026 development is not a §2870 case at all.** It is the January 1, 2024 enactment of **SB 699 and AB 1076**, which sharply expanded California's §16600 non-compete prohibition and — per multiple law-firm alerts — created a secondary lever for attacking overbroad PIIAAs as de facto non-competes. This is the *Whitewater* reasoning extended by statute.
- **The D3 letter template does NOT need structural revision before filing.** The product-category framing (Thedi is newsletter-drafting, not IAM) remains the correct primary argument on the current record. Two sharpening edits are recommended, not a rewrite: (a) replace the pre-2020 *Applera/Cubic* cite pair with a *Whitewater* cite, which is stronger and more current; and (b) soften the §2872-notice paragraph, because California courts have "thus far refused to punish employers who fail to properly give notice" under §2872 — so that paragraph as currently drafted overstates an available lever.

## 1. California §2870 Decisions, 2024–2026

**Finding: No published California state appellate decision or California
Supreme Court decision in this window directly interprets §2870's
"relate to" or "demonstrably anticipated R&D" language.**

Search channels used: Justia, FindLaw, Leagle, CourtListener-adjacent public
indexes, Law360 summaries accessible via public secondary sources, and the
employment-law practice-area client alerts of Orrick, Gibson Dunn, Fisher
Phillips, Cooley, Wilson Sonsini, Manatt, Hunton, Fenwick, Paul Weiss, and
several California boutique firms (Hirschfeld Kraemer, Kunzler Bean, Boutin
Jones, Fennemore).

The controlling case remains:

- **Whitewater West Industries, Ltd. v. Alleshouse**, 981 F.3d 1045 (Fed. Cir. 2020) — *summary only from secondary sources; verify with attorney*. Holding (as summarized by multiple law-firm alerts): an employment-agreement provision requiring an employee (Alleshouse, a surf-simulation designer at Wave Loch / Whitewater) to assign post-employment inventions that did not use the employer's trade secrets or confidential information was void as a §16600 restraint on trade. The Federal Circuit reasoned the assignment clause was "unlimited in time and geography" and reached inventions "merely suggested by" the prior employment.
    - Primary opinion on FindLaw: https://caselaw.findlaw.com/court/us-federal-circuit/2096871.html
    - Practitioner summary (ArentFox Schiff): https://www.afslaw.com/perspectives/alerts/federal-circuit-court-appeals-makes-waves-post-employment-invention-assignment
    - Practitioner summary (Manatt): https://www.manatt.com/insights/newsletters/intellectual-property-law/california-law-former-employer-cannot-require-assi
    - Practitioner summary (Hirschfeld Kraemer): https://www.hkemploymentlaw.com/invention-agreements-can-be-unenforceable-covenants-not-to-compete/
- **Why *Whitewater* matters to Thedi, even though it is a post-employment case.** *Whitewater* is doctrinally about §16600 non-compete enforcement, not §2870's subject-matter-relatedness test. But the *reasoning* — that California has a strong public policy against overbroad assignment clauses that reach beyond the employer's legitimate trade-secret interest — is routinely cited by plaintiff-side employment counsel to press courts toward a narrow reading of §2870's "relate to" exception. That narrow reading is exactly what Ashish's letter relies on.

Older precedent still cited in treatises and practitioner materials:

- *Cadence Design Systems, Inc. v. Bhandari*, 2007 WL 3343085 (N.D. Cal. 2007) — *summary only* — a district-court decision declining to enforce a §2870-overreaching PIIAA. Cited by secondary sources as an example of the "relate to" test being applied narrowly. Probably not strong enough to cite in a formal letter.
- *Cubic Corp. v. Marty*, 185 Cal. App. 3d 438 (1986) — *summary only* — the iter-2 D3 cite. Still sometimes cited in 2024-2026 law-firm alerts for its baseline recitation of §2870's elements, but rarely as the headline case. It is not "overruled"; it is simply older.
    - Justia: https://law.justia.com/cases/california/court-of-appeal/3d/185/438.html
- *Applera Corp.-Applied Biosystems Grp. v. Illumina, Inc.*, 375 F. App'x 12 (Fed. Cir. 2010) — *summary only* — the iter-2 D3 cite. Unpublished Federal Circuit decision; minor persuasive weight. No 2024-2026 case has reaffirmed or distinguished it in a published opinion.
    - Summary: https://www.leagle.com/decision/infco20100325236

## 2. Secondary Legal Sources, 2024–2026

**Key 2024 statutory change: California SB 699 and AB 1076** (both effective
January 1, 2024) — tightened §16600 non-compete prohibitions and required
employers to notify current and former employees by Feb. 14, 2024 that any
non-compete provision was void. Why this matters for §2870: *multiple law-
firm alerts* (Cooley, Wilson Sonsini, Paul Weiss, Fisher Phillips, Quinn
Emanuel) recommended that California employers "review PIIAAs" for any
clause that functions as a de facto non-compete — including any invention-
assignment clause reaching beyond the employer's legitimate §2870 scope.

## 3. AI-Specific §2870 Precedent, 2024–2026

**Finding: No reported California decision in this window has applied §2870
to an AI, LLM, or agentic-system side-project dispute.**

This is consistent with how slowly these disputes surface: PIIAA fights over
software side-projects typically appear as (a) preliminary-injunction motions
in D.Ct.-N.D. Cal. trade-secret cases, (b) breach-of-contract cross-claims in
patent-assignment disputes, and (c) arbitration proceedings that never enter
the public record. The Scale AI / Taskers misclassification litigation (filed
Dec 2024) is about worker classification, not invention ownership.

Practitioner commentary on AI-output ownership in employment contexts
(Peter Law Group, Jan 2026; terms.law forum thread, 2026) flags a different
risk to Ashish's situation: **commingling of work and personal AI sessions.**
If Ashish used a Saviynt-issued Claude/Copilot/ChatGPT account, or
a personal account but signed in on a Saviynt device, a hostile GC could
argue the output was "developed using employer equipment or facilities"
and is outside §2870's safe harbor. This is not a new legal rule; it is a
new factual pattern that old §2870 law applies cleanly to. The iter-2 D3
letter template already addresses this correctly in Section 4(b) by naming
every third-party service Thedi uses (GitHub, Butterbase, IonRouter, Resend)
and confirming each is accessed via personal accounts. Keep that.

- **Peter Law Group 2026 commentary**: https://peterlawgroup.com/a-cautionary-tale-ai-intellectual-property-and-what-california-employment-law-has-to-do-with-it/ — general tone-setter, no specific §2870 case.

## 4. The §2872 Notification-to-Employee Requirement

**Finding: California courts have "thus far refused to punish employers who
fail to properly give notice under §2872."** This is the most important
single finding in this research pass, because it directly contradicts an
assumption in how iter-2 D3's Section 5 is drafted.

- **§2872 text**: https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=LAB&sectionNum=2872.
- **Secondary source confirming judicial non-enforcement**: Sierra IP Law, https://sierraiplaw.com/employee-invention/ (summarizing that courts have declined to hold §2870 assignment provisions unenforceable solely because the employer failed to deliver §2872 notice).

Practical implication for Thedi's letter: the iter-2 D3 Section 5 paragraph
that references §2872 ("this letter is responsive to it") is **fine as a
factual recitation** but should NOT be offered to Saviynt as a legal lever
— because Saviynt's counsel will know it isn't one. The defensive value of
mentioning §2872 is social (it shows Ashish knows his rights); the offensive
value is small. No revision needed to the sentence itself, but Ashish should
not rely on §2872 non-compliance as a fallback argument if push came to shove.

No 2024-2026 California case has changed this judicial posture.

## 5. Synthesis for Ashish

### Have the iter-2 D3 cases been reaffirmed, distinguished, or overruled?

- ***Applera*** — neither reaffirmed nor distinguished in any published 2024-2026 decision. It is an unpublished 2010 Federal Circuit opinion; it was always a weak cite. It is still on the books.
- ***Cubic Corp. v. Marty*** — the 1986 state appellate decision — is still good law. It is still routinely cited in practitioner materials for its baseline elements recitation. Not the strongest modern authority, but not stale.
- **Neither has been overruled.** Neither has been strengthened by a 2024-2026 decision. Their usefulness in a formal letter remains exactly what it was in iter-2 D3: an "in accord" citation, not a controlling holding.

### Is the product-category framing still the right defense?

**Yes, and the case for it has gotten marginally stronger, not weaker,
since iter-2 D3.** The 2024 statutory tightening of §16600 (SB 699, AB
1076) and the continued weight of *Whitewater* both push California law
toward a narrow reading of what "relates to the employer's business."
The product-category argument — Thedi is newsletter-drafting infrastructure,
Saviynt is IAM — fits that narrow reading. The alternative defenses
(time-and-place-of-creation, resource-use) remain necessary but not
sufficient on their own; the product-category argument is what does the
heavy lifting if a hostile GC tries to pull Thedi into "anticipated R&D"
because Saviynt blogs about "AI agents writing code."

Iter-16 A's factual finding — that Saviynt's public R&D scope is
IAM-governance-of-agents, not building DevOps/SRE agents — is the
argument that wins the product-category framing. That factual record
is what the letter should lead with, supported by *Whitewater* as
secondary authority.

### 2024-2026 citations to ADD to the letter template

Three specific, surgical edits (not a rewrite):

1. **Replace the *Applera/Cubic* pair in Section 5** with a *Whitewater* primary cite and a *Cubic* secondary cite. Suggested text (verify with attorney): *"California law disfavors overbroad employer claims on employee inventions that fall outside the employer's legitimate trade-secret and business scope. See Whitewater West Industries, Ltd. v. Alleshouse, 981 F.3d 1045 (Fed. Cir. 2020) (California-law assignment provision reaching post-employment inventions beyond the employer's trade-secret interest void under §16600); Cubic Corp. v. Marty, 185 Cal. App. 3d 438 (1986) (reciting §2870 elements)."*
2. **Add a footnote or parenthetical** referencing California Bus. & Prof. Code §16600 as tightened by SB 699 / AB 1076 (effective Jan. 1, 2024). This signals Ashish's counsel is current. Suggested: *"See also Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code §16600, as amended by SB 699 and AB 1076 (2024 Cal. Stat.)."*
3. **Soften Section 5's §2872 framing** — keep the §2872 reference for factual context, but remove any implication that §2872 non-compliance would defeat Saviynt's assignment claim. Current courts don't back that up.

### Procedural updates affecting delivery, acknowledgment, or archival

No new 2024-2026 procedural rulings. The iter-2 D3 delivery protocol
(personal address, certified mail, countersignature block, 14-day response
window, cc to HR and hiring manager) remains the standard California
employment-counsel playbook. One addition worth considering:

- **Archive outbound in a way that survives Saviynt laptop seizure.** Store
  the filed letter, tracking number, and any countersigned response in a
  personal cloud account (not Saviynt's), and in a physical folder offsite.
  This is general defensive hygiene, not §2870-specific, but the 2024-2025
  rise in employer-issued-device remote-wipe capabilities makes it
  materially more important than it was five years ago.
